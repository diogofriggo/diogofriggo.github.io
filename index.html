2009

First job!

Opportunities to work with software development for unexperienced developers (however academically qualified) were scarce at the time - there wasn't such a shortage of talent as is common nowadays.

I pushed my luck and it worked! They said they did indeed need someone to translate documents and help with talking to local clients both in-person and via Skype.

Now I just needed to do a good job and convince them that an academic programmer could do commercial work, so I found the perfect opportunity to show my qualifications: student to business...

2010-2013

Software developer at HCL

I had many very competent, motivated and experienced colleagues to learn from. What I learned from them was worth every single ounce of hard work to get there. But the person who enabled me to grow the most was Alexandre Klaser. He had a huge impact not only on my career but also on my personal life. I admire him to this day.

While some would question whether an inexperienced dev was a right fit for the project, Alexandre would trust me with more and more responsibilities, to the point that after 1 year the once inexperienced dev would then be in charge in defining and mantaining the architecture of new projects and addressing doubts from more experienced developers

Like many companies at the time we were trying to push for Agile adoption. And like many companies we failed on some respects but succeeded in many others. One of the most gratifying feedback from a client went like this:

We've been trying to build this inventory management system for years and everytime on the delivery day we'd find out that what we got is not what we wanted. Your are the first company to deliver what we asked for

That was not by accident. We delivered biweekly. By the final delivery stakeholders would have had plenty of opportunities to say that we were not building what they wanted. That's why we succeeded!

We tried to follow Agile by the book (or should I say by the manifesto) but reality, company- and client-specific issues presented many unforeseen challenges. I had a wonderful team that acted in good-faith and with the client's interest in mind and heart and managed to overcome every obstacle. I think it's fair to say that Agile provided the framework and the people, the engine.

Aug 2013

Collaborative keyboard!

After getting to know PUC university from my time in the S2B program, I started to watch some of their open lectures on software.
One of them in particular caught my attention. It was a lecture on how NodeJS could be used to reverse the HTTP model, that is, actively sending a response to clients without them having initiated a request in the first place!
Nowadays this is known as the WebSocket protocol, but it was exciting news back then.
Inspired by the lecture I built a collaborative keyboard later on that day.
The project is still live, check it out
Try playing the keyboard from two different devices and see how the sound is routed back to both
Source code on my github

2013

The path to UFRGS

In this year UFRGS would feature as best federal university for the 2nd time in a row and would keep the crown for 6 more years! That's where I wanted to study some sort of applied mathematics.
While researching which course was the best fit to what I wanted to learn I found Physics Engineering - a perfect match!

Admission on my previous university was very easy. Being admitted in one of the best universities of the country was an entirely different challenge.
Even though I had improved a lot on my math skills I knew I still had a very poor background on every other subject like History, Chemistry, Biology, Literature... well, basically everything else. That was so because the level of teaching in public schools where I grew up is light-years behind what's required to join higher-level studies.
So even though I have always been a good student and had excellent grades I knew that coming from a public school I had a lot of catching up to do.

I decided to focus all my attention on it so I quit my job, took the money I had and payed for a full-year preparation course to UFRGS's fiercely competetitive admission exams (much like SATs) which cover in-depth every subject learned in school.
I used my remaining savings to support myself during the whole year, frequently having to make some hard choices on priorites like having enough money for commute and food or buying required books.

2014-2018

Physics Engineering!

After a full year of preparation culminating in four long days of admission exams I fulfilled the dream of getting into this university!
Physics engineering was a very new course. When I joined, there wasn't any graduates yet. However, the curriculum and the idea behind the course was enough to dispel any doubts.

The idea was simple: just as a mechanical engineer applies well-know physics established some 4 centuries ago and an electrical engineer applies physics known for over 2 centuries, a physics engineer would be in a position to apply 20th-century-onwards physics into solving real-life issues, particularly ones involving cutting-edge technology. Keep in mind the nearly exponential growth of science output before thinking the physics engineer has less to learn!

Since science is built upon earlier work rather than replacing it, for the first two years we learned the foundations common to all engineering courses such as:

  • Foundational Physics
  • Calculus
  • Technical drawing
  • Fundamental Chemistry
  • Linear Algebra
  • Probality and Statistics
  • Classical Mechanics
  • Electromagnetic Theory
  • Applied Mathematics (think Fourier transforms and Vector analysis)
  • Differential Equations Theory
  • Thermodynamics

The physics engineer also learns about a bunch of other subjects:

  • Mathematical Methods (think calculus in the complex plane, green's functions)
  • Nanotechnology
  • Photonics
  • Biophysics
  • Computational Methods
  • Quantum Mechanics (perhaps the toughest subject - we joke that this is the course's turning point)
  • Analog and digital eletronics
  • Instrumentation
  • Radiation and Matter Interaction
  • Econophysics
  • Spectroscopy
  • Semiconductors
  • Microprocessors
  • Statistical Mechanics
  • Solid State Theory
  • Renewable Energy

I can't list everything out here or you'll be bored to death. Let me just say that it's 5 intense years of studying and certainly the best decision I've made in my academic career, what I learned at UFRGS is priceless, it opened up whole new worlds to me.
Additionally to the regular subjects UFRGS allows one to attend classes from different courses. I took a class on Particle Physics for physics graduates and one of the hardest classes in Mathematics: Analysis (certainly the hardest I've taken, ever)
